on how I work

Most people analyze data. I try to understand what the data is a symptom of. There is usually a more interesting problem hiding underneath the one everyone is looking at. That is what I find interesting: the invisible logic, the structural gap, the assumption nobody has questioned yet.

I am not a pure analyst, and I am not a pure strategist. I sit somewhere in the middle: someone who can get deep into the numbers and then zoom out to ask what any of it means for the business, the product, or the person using it.

I build things: quizzes, apps, case studies, models, because I think building is the fastest way to understand something. Analysis tells you what is there. Building tells you what is possible.

I care about behavior. How people actually make decisions (not how we think they do). How products create habits or break them. How data reflects human patterns, not just business ones.

On Curiosity

On Matcha, Systems,
and Why I Build

Matcha is a ritual, not just a drink. There is something satisfying about the way the whisking works: the same motion, producing a different result every time depending on temperature, pressure, attention. It is a small system. And I like understanding small systems.

When I built MatchaMeki, it was not really about matcha. It was about asking: can the things we like, the textures, the temperatures, the quiet preferences, tell us something true about who we are? And can that be turned into a product people actually want to use?

That is the same instinct behind every case study, every SQL query, every analysis I have built. Find the thing that seems small but is not. Build something around it. See what it reveals. That is not a methodology. It is just how I think.

Mindset

How I Think

01

I find invisible problems

The most interesting problems are not obvious; they are structural. They are hiding in the assumptions everyone accepts, the metrics nobody questions, the gap between what the data says and what the business is actually doing. I look for those.

02

I think in systems, not tasks

A task produces an output. Systems thinking asks: what are the feedback loops here? What breaks when this changes? What is the second-order effect? I naturally work at the level of structure, not just execution.

03

I build to understand

Analysis is one way of knowing something. Building is another: faster, more honest, harder to fake. When I build, I am not showing off a skill. I am running an experiment. The product is just how I take notes.

04

I connect data with human behavior

Numbers are always a proxy for something people did. I want to understand both: the pattern in the data, and the behavior that created it. That is where the most useful insight lives, in the translation.
